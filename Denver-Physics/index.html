What I originally had in mind here was a discussion group dedicated to the "philosophical foundations" of various areas in physics. Topics might include, for example, the "ontological status of the wave function" or the "measurement problem" in quantum theory. This kind of discussion requires serious knowledge of mathematics and physics, and at least some familiarity with the technical terminology used by philosophers of science.

It turned out that quite a few people simply wanted to have a forum for discussing and learning a bit more about physics per se. Apparently there is currently no other group in the area that serves this purpose. That's why I changed the name to the more generic "Denver Physics Study Group." My main personal interest is in the interpretation (what the theories are telling us about "reality") of quantum theory and relativistic physics. I certainly won't try to restrict discussion to my personal favorites. I will however, outline some of the questions I'm interested in, as best I understand them.

Questions

The ontological status the quantum state

Does the quantum state (sometimes called the "wave function") represent something in the "real" world? Or is it just a calculational tool or a "measure of our ignorance" about ... something? Another way to ask this question would be: What is a superposition really? Note that every state is a superposition in some basis.

The measurement problem

(Non-relativistic) quantum mechanics provides a continuous, deterministic formula for the evolution of a quantum state, via the Schroedinger equation. Until a "measurement" happens. Then everything changes. But there is no general agreement on when or how a measurement occurs. In fact many people suspect that a "state changing" measurement is a fiction. Many of the various "interpretations" of QM are the result of trying to solve this problem in various ways.

Locality

Did John Bell "prove" that quantum physics is non-local, therefore the world is non-local? Or did he rather show that if we try to replace quantum theory with a classical theory, then that classical theory must be non-local? Does this just turn out to be a disagreement about the definition of "locality" or is there a deeper debate happening?

General Physics Discussion/Study Group

As I mentioned above, as soon as I started the "Philosophy of Physics" group I started hearing from people who didn't have all that much background in math or physics but were interested in learning more about it. For people who didn't major in a scientific discipline in school, but are genuinely interested, I guess there's no clear path to getting any "serious" information about many of the interesting things currently going on in physics.

It seems that there may be a need for a middle ground between reading popular physics books (arguably ineffective) and going "back to school" to take the required math and physics classes (arguably impractical).

The irony of the situation is that it is possible to learn quite a bit, for example to gain a really basic (but correct and quantitative) understanding of, say, quantum theory and special relativity -- without spending years at it. It is my believe that anyone who has a good grasp of high school algebra can get to "square one" in these subjects with about 6 months of study (say 5 or 6 hours a week).

Maybe one of the things we can do in this group is to make people aware of what resources there are for doing this, and let people who have some knowledge do a bit of mentoring for people who are interested in learning.
